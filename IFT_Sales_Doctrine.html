The Sales & Marketing Leader IFT Needs — A Character Definition

The right person is not a closer. They are a strategist who knows when not to close.

The five defining qualities

1. Never blames price for a lost deal. If the answer is always "we were undercut," the problem is not the market — it is the salesperson's inability to build perceived value. The right candidate walks away from a price war before it starts, because they have already repositioned the conversation around something the competitor cannot match.

2. Knows when to walk away — and does. Retreating from the wrong opportunity is a skill, not a failure. The right person can read a situation, conclude that this client, this deal, or this market is not one IFT should win, and exit cleanly. That discipline protects the organisation from the hidden cost of bad contracts far more than any new revenue they bring in.

3. Defines what a good customer looks like — and screens against it. Before pursuing anyone, they build a clear picture of IFT's ideal client: the sector, the decision-making structure, the problem complexity, the willingness to pay for integration depth rather than headcount. Then they use that picture to disqualify leads early, so no one wastes six months on a prospect who was never going to convert.

4. Can read the true intent of a decision-maker. In Japanese corporate culture, "that sounds interesting" frequently means "no." In Indian enterprise procurement, the person who attends the meeting is often not the person who holds the decision. The right candidate navigates both. They can sit across the table from a managing director who is giving positive signals and know, from what is not said, whether the intent is real.

5. Builds differentiated proposals, not catalogues. They do not present IFT's capabilities as a list. They present a specific answer to a specific problem that only IFT can deliver in the way IFT delivers it. Every proposal is designed to make comparison with a competitor structurally difficult.

The underlying philosophy

These five qualities share a common root: the discipline to refuse the wrong battles.

Not competing in Red Oceans. Not chasing clients who will commoditise the relationship the moment the contract is signed. Not defending products or market positions that have no future. Not mistaking activity for progress.

This is a strategist, not a salesperson. The distinction matters enormously at IFT's current inflection point — because what IFT does not pursue in the next two years will define its trajectory just as much as what it does.
