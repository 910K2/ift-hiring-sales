The deciding factor today

The question is not who wrote the better report. It is: who can change their recommendation under pressure, explain why, and still walk out of the room with the conversation going forward? That is the Sales strategist IFT needs. The interview questions below are designed to surface exactly that.

How to use this guide
Questions are sequenced: open with the report → probe the constraint → test the Doctrine → close with the transition reality. For each question, "Strong answer" shows what a strategist says. "Concern signal" shows what a closer says. The gap between those two is your hiring filter.
Opening — for both candidates
Q0 — Opening · Both
"Before we go into questions — take two minutes. Tell me one thing in your report you wish you had done differently, and why."
What this surfaces
Self-awareness and intellectual honesty. A strategist will immediately identify the gap (Tanya: India was thin / Kunal: drone recommendation was contradictory). A closer will say "I'm happy with it" or make cosmetic comments about slide design.
Strong answer
Names a substantive gap, explains what they would have done instead, and shows they understand why it mattered for the assignment.
Concern: "I would have added more data" or "the design could be better." Surface-level.
Tanya — specific questions
Q1 — Tanya · India Gap
"Your report analyses FIG as a group. But the assignment was India market entry. Walk me through your thinking on India specifically — what did you find most difficult to figure out, and where did you land?"

Q2 — Tanya · Constraint Test
"You included Drone-as-a-Service and Smart City government contracts as growth levers. We discussed that drone manufacturing requires 60% local content — which IFT cannot manage right now. And government tenders at city scale take 2–3 years. Does that change your recommendation? What would you remove?"
Strong answer
Immediately withdraws those recommendations. Refocuses on fleet MDVR and KTS hotel as the two viable near-term paths. Does not defend the original output under pressure.
Concern: "These are long-term opportunities worth keeping on the roadmap." Inability to discard under pressure is a Doctrine failure.
Q3 — Tanya · Specificity
"Your 90-day plan says identify 50 high-value prospects. Give me three company names — specific Indian companies — that IFT should approach first for fleet telematics. And tell me why those three."
Strong answer
Names companies she actually knows — Delhivery, Blue Dart, GATI, VRL Logistics, Mahindra Logistics, or similar. Explains the fit: fleet size, sector, openness to IoT. Shows real market knowledge not borrowed from a global market report.
Concern: Names generic categories ("large logistics companies") or global names (Amazon, Flipkart) without specific reasoning. Report-level thinking, not sales-level thinking.
Q4 — Tanya · Withdrawal Experience
"Tell me about a deal or a client you chose to walk away from — not because the budget was zero, but because something about the engagement told you it was wrong. What did you see, and what did you do?"
Strong answer
A specific story with: a clear diagnosis of why the client was wrong (values, procurement culture, expectations mismatch), the internal pressure she faced to keep pursuing, and what validated the decision in retrospect. The Escort sensor example she mentioned in the first interview should surface here with more detail.
Concern: Story where the client walked away first, or where "walking away" meant stopping follow-up emails. Passive withdrawal, not strategic withdrawal.

Q2 — Kunal · AIS-140 Clarification
"You cited AIS-140 as a demand driver for IFT's fleet product. AIS-140 mandates basic GPS tracking for public transport — it does not require MDVR. How does IFT's MDVR differentiate from an AIS-140 compliant GPS device — and who is the actual buyer?"
Strong answer
Acknowledges the distinction immediately. Correctly identifies that MDVR's value is above AIS-140 compliance — driver safety, fuel fraud evidence, AI video analysis, route intelligence. The buyer is the fleet operator seeking accountability and cost reduction, not just regulatory compliance.
Concern: Defends the AIS-140 framing or cannot distinguish between the two products. Technical misunderstanding that would hurt credibility in sales conversations with fleet CTOs.
Q3 — Kunal · From Analysis to Action
"Your buyer psychology section says relationship-first culture — business is built on trust before contracts. Give me a specific example from your own sales experience where you had to build that trust from zero with an Indian enterprise buyer. How long did it take, what did you do, and what was the first signal that the relationship had actually formed?"
Strong answer
A specific named story — not a framework. The timeline is honest (months, not weeks). The trust signal is concrete and human — a conversation that shifted from formal to informal, an introduction to a colleague, a call that wasn't scheduled. Shows the theory has been lived, not just read.
Concern: Describes a process ("I did regular follow-ups and sent case studies") rather than a relationship. Academic answer to a human question.
Q4 — Kunal · Web3 to Telematics
"Your last two roles were in Web3 and blockchain — products that are intangible, speculative, and sold largely on vision. IFT's products are physical, embedded in vehicles, and sold on reliability and RCA depth. What do you expect to be hardest about making that shift — and how do you plan to close that gap in the first 90 days?"
Strong answer
Honest about the gap. Names something specific — "I have never been on a fleet operator's premises, I don't know the physical reality of what breaks and why." Plans concrete steps: ride-alongs with the IFT technical team, reading protocol documentation, visiting a client site in the first 30 days. Shows the gap is understood, not minimised.
Concern: "Selling complex technology is selling complex technology." Equivalence argument — does not acknowledge the domain shift is real and significant.
Closing — for both candidates
Q5 — Closing · Both · Transition Honesty
"One thing I want to be direct about: IFT is mid-transition. When you join, you will see the gap between where we are today and where InfoTrack X is going. Some of our current service quality and product positioning is still being rebuilt. What specifically would concern you most about joining in that state — and what would you need from leadership to make it work?"
Strong answer
Names real concerns — being asked to sell a product that isn't ready, internal resistance to the new direction, unclear commission structure during transition, no established playbook. Then asks for something specific from leadership: clear mandate, access to the technical team, honest communication when the product isn't ready. Shows they have thought about what they are actually walking into.
The signal we are looking for
Does the transparency make them more interested or less? A strategist who understands turnarounds will lean in. A closer who needs a finished platform to sell will show hesitation. Both reactions are valid data — the wrong reaction is no reaction at all.
Concern: "I love a challenge, I'm not worried." No concerns expressed. Either not listening or performing enthusiasm. Both are problems.
The final test — after the session ends

Ask yourself one question: Did this person say anything that surprised me — something I had not considered, a reframe I did not expect, a question that showed they were thinking beyond the assignment?

A strategist leaves you with something new. A salesperson leaves you feeling good about the conversation. IFT needs the former. The transition ahead requires someone who will push back on the wrong direction before the contract is signed — not after.
