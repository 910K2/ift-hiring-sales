Sales & Marketing Hire — Second Interview Guide

To: Praveen HG (COO)  ·  From: Office of the MD  ·  April 2026  ·  Confidential

1. Why one candidate, not two

I appreciate your instinct to move on both candidates — it reflects the right sense of urgency. However, my recommendation is to select one, and here is the reasoning.

A Sales and Marketing leader at this stage of IFT's transition is not a headcount decision. It is a doctrine decision. The person we hire will define how IFT approaches the market, which clients it pursues, and which it refuses. Two people in that role simultaneously — especially across two cities — will produce two interpretations of the strategy before the strategy has even taken root. Accountability will blur. One strong hire, given full ownership, will move faster and more coherently than two who are still calibrating against each other.

If both candidates pass the second interview at a high bar, we can revisit the second hire in six months — by which point the first person will have established the playbook the second can follow. That is a stronger foundation than parallel onboarding from zero.

On NOIDA vs. Bangalore: For the first hire, Bangalore is the stronger choice. The candidate will need to work closely with you, Bhopal, and Shaik during the build phase. Physical proximity matters in the first six months. NOIDA — and the Delhi/NCR defence and government sector access that comes with it — is a valid argument for the second hire, once the first has established the model.

2. The profile in brief — what we are selecting for

The right person is not a closer. They are a strategist who knows when not to close. That distinction is the entire hiring filter.

QualityWhat it looks like in practice
1. Never blames price Repositions the conversation to value before a price war begins. Walks away rather than discounting.
2. Knows when to retreat — and does Has a concrete example of a deal they chose not to pursue, and can explain the logic clearly.
3. Defines and screens the ideal customer Can articulate who IFT's client is NOT, before being asked. Uses that filter proactively.
4. Reads decision-maker intent Understands that "sounds interesting" in Japanese contexts often means no. Can navigate Indian procurement's gap between title and actual authority.
5. Builds differentiated proposals Does not present a capability list. Presents a specific answer to a specific problem that only IFT can deliver in the way IFT delivers it.
The underlying philosophy across all five: the discipline to refuse the wrong battles. Not competing in Red Oceans. Not chasing clients who will commoditise the relationship the moment the contract is signed. Not defending products or market positions that have no future. Not mistaking activity for progress.

This is a strategist, not a salesperson. The distinction matters enormously at IFT's current inflection point.

3. A candid note to share with both candidates

Before the second interview, both candidates should be told the following — honestly and directly. The person we want will respect the transparency. The wrong candidate will be deterred by it, which is useful information.

What to tell them:

"IFT is in the middle of a transition. We are moving from an outsourcing supplier model to a Japan-to-India product and solution partner model. That shift is real and it is already underway — but it is not complete. When you join, you will see the gap between where we are and where we are going. Some of our current products and service quality standards are still being rebuilt. We are telling you this directly because we want someone who can help us make the transition, not someone who arrives expecting a finished platform. The role requires both the vision to see what IFT is becoming and the tolerance to operate through the imperfect present."

The commodity-to-differentiation transition is genuinely hard. It requires changing products, pricing logic, service quality standards, and — most difficult of all — the internal mindset of a team that has operated in a different model for years. The Sales leader will encounter that friction from day one. We owe them the honesty of saying so in advance.

4. Second interview — question guide

The session should run approximately 60–75 minutes. You and I conduct it together. The questions are sequenced to move from general philosophy to specific judgment to live application.

Opening framing (5 minutes): Hand the candidate the Sales Doctrine document and the website mockup. Give them five minutes to read in silence. No explanation. Watch how they engage with the material — whether they read carefully or skim, whether they take notes, whether they ask a clarifying question or wait passively.

Question 1 — Doctrine comprehension & self-awareness
"Having read this, which of the five points do you personally find most difficult to execute — and why? Give me a real example."
What we are looking for: Intellectual honesty and self-awareness. "All five are important" or "I agree with everything" is a disqualifying answer. The right candidate identifies genuine personal tension — perhaps between walking away from a deal and hitting a quarterly target, or between reading a decision-maker's true intent and the pressure to move fast. The example must be specific and from their own experience.

Biz School angle: If they cite negotiation frameworks (BATNA, anchoring, the Harvard method), push immediately: "Give me a situation where the textbook said one thing and the client's actual behaviour required something different." We want to know if the theory has been stress-tested in the field.
Question 2 — The retreat test
"Tell me about a significant deal you chose to walk away from. What made you decide to leave, how did you make that call, and what happened to that client or competitor who took it on?"
What we are looking for: A candidate who has never walked away from a deal has never had the authority or the conviction to do so. Both are problems. The story should include: a clear diagnosis of why the deal was wrong for their organisation, the internal resistance they faced in making that call (management pressure, quota pressure), and ideally — what validated the decision in retrospect. If the competitor who took the deal later lost it badly, even better.

Biz School angle: Ask how they quantified the opportunity cost of pursuing the wrong deal versus the cost of walking away. Did they frame it as a resource allocation decision? A risk assessment? The analytical language they use tells us how they think.
Question 3 — Ideal customer definition
"Based on everything you have read and heard about IFT — who is NOT our customer? Give me three specific types of client or engagement that IFT should actively avoid, and explain why each one is a trap."
What we are looking for: This is the most revealing question on the list. Almost anyone can say who a good customer is. Defining who to exclude — with precision and commercial reasoning — is the mark of someone who thinks strategically about market positioning. We are looking for answers that go beyond the obvious (e.g. "clients with no budget") to structural insight: clients who will commoditise the relationship, clients whose procurement process prevents value-based pricing, clients whose technical requirements would force IFT back into the silo model it is exiting.

If they struggle: Ask "What kind of client would make you feel, six months into the engagement, that you should never have signed them?" That reframe often unlocks more honest thinking.
Question 4 — Reading the room (Japan + India dual context)
"Describe a situation where a senior stakeholder — a client, a partner, or an internal decision-maker — was giving you positive signals but you sensed something was wrong. How did you read it, what did you do, and were you right?"
What we are looking for: In Japanese business culture, surface agreement frequently conceals fundamental reservation. In Indian enterprise procurement, the person in the room is often not the person who decides. We need someone who has developed genuine antennae for the gap between stated and actual intent — and who has acted on that reading, not just noticed it.

Biz School angle: If they reference negotiation theory (active listening, mirroring, silence as a tool), ask: "When did using silence cost you something, and when did it save a deal?" Technique without situational judgment is not enough.
Question 5 — Live application (website mockup)
"You have seen our website mockup. In 60 seconds — without preparing — tell me what message it currently sends to a prospective Japanese industrial partner. Then tell me what is missing."
What we are looking for: Speed of commercial reading and the ability to synthesise positioning under pressure. The 60-second constraint is deliberate — it removes the ability to construct a careful answer and reveals instinct. A strong candidate will identify both what works (the technical authority angle) and what is absent (a specific client story, a proof point, a reason to believe). A weak candidate will describe the design or say "it looks professional."

Follow-up: "If you were joining IFT next month, what is the first thing you would change about how we present ourselves to the market — and why that first?"
Question 6 — Transition honesty
"We told you earlier that IFT is mid-transition — the gap between current reality and the target model is real and you will see it from day one. What specifically would concern you most about joining an organisation in that state, and what would you need from leadership to make it work?"
What we are looking for: This question filters for self-awareness, realism, and the kind of psychological resilience the role actually requires. A candidate who says "no concerns, I love a challenge" has not understood the question. A candidate who lists concerns thoughtfully — internal resistance to the new model, unclear product-market fit for the transition period, the risk of being asked to sell something that is not yet ready — and then articulates what support they would need, is showing exactly the strategic judgment the role demands.

This is also a test of how they handle honesty from a potential employer. If our transparency about the transition makes them more interested, that is the right instinct. If it visibly unsettles them, that tells us something important.

5. Scoring guidance

On doctrine Identifies genuine personal difficulty with specifics "I agree with all five points"
On retreat Has a real story, including the internal friction of making the call Has never walked away, or can only cite cases where the client walked first
On ideal customer Exclusion criteria are structural and commercially reasoned Exclusions are obvious (no budget, wrong size) — no strategic depth
On intent-reading Specific story, acted on the reading, outcome validates the judgment Theory without a real example; or example where they ignored the signal
On website Immediately identifies positioning gap, not design detail Comments on aesthetics; cannot articulate the buyer's perspective
On transition Names real concerns and asks for what they need — then commits No concerns expressed; or concerns expressed with no path forward
The overall test: After 75 minutes, ask yourself — did this person say anything that surprised me? Did they push back on anything, reframe anything, or show me a way of thinking I had not considered? A strategist leaves you with something new. A salesperson leaves you feeling good. We are hiring for the former.
